Topic-Based Watermarks for Large Language Models

2024 Preprint

Authors: Alexander Nemecek, Yuzhou Jiang, Erman Ayday

TL;DR: This paper proposes a topic-based watermarking scheme for large language models that partitions vocabulary into semantically aligned token subsets rather than random lists. We demonstrate that our method achieves comparable text quality to industry systems like Google's SynthID-Text while providing enhanced robustness against paraphrasing and lexical perturbation attacks, offering a lightweight approach that requires minimal computational overhead and facilitates straightforward adoption in production pipelines.

Watermarking Without Standards Is Not AI Governance

2025 Preprint

Authors: Alexander Nemecek, Yuzhou Jiang, Erman Ayday

TL;DR: This paper argues that current watermarking for AI-generated content lacks the technical robustness and standardization needed for effective governance, often failing basic transformations like paraphrasing. We propose a three-layer framework requiring technical standards, independent audits, and enforceable policies to transform watermarking from symbolic compliance into meaningful accountability.

ZKPROV: A Zero-Knowledge Approach to Dataset Provenance for Large Language Models

2025 Preprint

Authors: Mina Namazi, Alexander Nemecek, Erman Ayday

TL;DR: This paper introduces ZKPROV, a cryptographic framework that uses zero-knowledge proofs to verify that large language model responses derive from authorized datasets without revealing the dataset contents or model parameters. Unlike existing approaches that prove computational correctness, ZKPROV provides statistical guarantees that a model was trained on specific authenticated data through efficient recursive proof systems, achieving verification for practical deployment in regulated domains like healthcare.

The Feasibility of Topic-Based Watermarking on Academic Peer Reviews

2025 Preprint

Authors: Alexander Nemecek, Yuzhou Jiang, Erman Ayday

TL;DR: This paper evaluates topic-based watermarking (TBW) for detecting LLM-generated academic peer reviews, finding that it preserves review quality while maintaining strong detection accuracy even under paraphrasing attacks. Using authentic conference data, the study shows TBW can reliably identify machine-generated reviews across different model configurations without degrading semantic content, offering a practical solution for enforcing peer review policies that prohibit LLM assistance.

Cluster-Aware Attacks on Graph Watermarks

2025 Preprint

Authors: Alexander Nemecek, Emre Yilmaz, Erman Ayday

TL;DR: This paper introduces cluster-aware attacks on graph watermarks, where adversaries exploit community structure to strategically add/remove edges within or between clusters, degrading watermark detection by up to 80% more than random attacks. The authors propose a mitigation strategy that distributes watermark nodes across graph communities, improving attribution accuracy by up to 60% under attack while maintaining computational efficiency and structural integrity.
